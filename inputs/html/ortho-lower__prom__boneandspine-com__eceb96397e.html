Lower Extremity Functional Scale (LEFS)– Method, Scoring & Interpretation

  • Dr Arun Pal Singh
  • Advances in orthopedics

Last Updated on June 8, 2025

The Lower Extremity Functional Scale (LEFS) is a validated patient-reported outcome measure used to assess functional status of the lower limb in individuals with musculoskeletal conditions. It helps quantify a patient’s ability to perform common physical tasks and monitor their progress over time.

Lower Extremity Functional Scale is commonly used in orthopedic clinics, physiotherapy settings, and rehabilitation programs for conditions such as ACL injuries, total knee replacement, ankle sprains, and even neurological disorders like stroke. It is valued for its simplicity, responsiveness to clinical change, and strong test–retest reliability.

Lower Extremity Functional Scale was developed by Binkley et al. in 1999. It consists of 20 questions targeting everyday activities, ranging from walking and stair climbing to squatting and recreational movements.

The responses are scored and totaled to produce a functional percentage score, which reflects the patient’s lower extremity capability.

This article outlines the questionnaire, scoring method, interpretation strategies, and clinical use of LEFS.

The areas of Assessment in Lower Extremity Functional Scale

The scale is used for measuring lower extremity function in a wide variety of disorders and treatments. It is used to assess the following parameters.

  • Activities of daily living
  • Balance – non-vestibular coordination
  • Functional mobility
  • Life participation
  • Occupational performance
  • Quality of life
  • Range of motion and strength.

It can be used to monitor the patient over time and to evaluate the effectiveness of an intervention.

Method of Evaluating the Patient

LEFS Questionnaire

The Lower Extremity Functional Scale is administered as a self-reported questionnaire consisting of 20 items. Each item relates to a specific functional activity involving the lower limb, and the patient is asked to rate the degree of difficulty they experience while performing that activity.

The questionnaire is usually provided in paper or digital form. It is easy to complete and typically takes under five minutes.

Patients are asked to rate their ability to perform the following:

  1. Your usual work, housework, or school activities
  2. Hobbies, recreational, or sporting activities
  3. Getting into or out of the bath
  4. Walking between rooms
  5. Putting on shoes or socks
  6. Squatting
  7. Lifting an object like a bag of groceries from the floor
  8. Performing heavy activities around the home
  9. Performing light activities around the home
  10. Getting into or out of a car
  11. Walking two blocks
  12. Walking one mile
  13. Going up or down 10 stairs
  14. Standing for one hour
  15. Sitting for one hour
  16. Running on even ground
  17. Running on uneven ground
  18. Making sharp turns while running fast
  19. Hopping
  20. Rolling over in bed

The total score is calculated by summing the responses for all 20 items, with a maximum possible score of 80.

Tip: Ensure the patient is responding based on their current functional status, not projected or desired performance.

Scoring and Interpretation

After the patient completes the Lower Extremity Functional Scale, the total score is obtained by summing the scores for each of the 20 questions. [1]

  • Maximum total score: 80 (indicates no difficulty with any activity)
  • Minimum total score: 0 (unable to perform any activity)

Converting to Percentage

For easier interpretation and tracking over time, the total score can be converted into a percentage using the formula:

LEFS % Score = (Total Score / 80) × 100

Examples:

  • A score of 80 → 100% functional
  • A score of 64 → (64/80) × 100 = 80%
  • A score of 40 → 50% function

Reliability

The test–retest reliability of the LEFS has been reported at 0.94, indicating excellent consistency across repeated assessments. [2]

The limitation of the scale is that it is not possible to assess the status of pre-disease physical function. Because the answers to the questions that pertain to past status are based on the recall and it brings in recall bias.

Clinical Use Cases of LEFS

The Lower Extremity Functional Scale (LEFS) is routinely used across orthopedic, neurological, and rehabilitation settings to evaluate lower limb function in patients with varying degrees of disability.

Post-Surgical Rehabilitation

After procedures such as anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) reconstruction, total knee replacement, hip arthroplasty, and ankle surgery.

It provides a structured method to assess progressive return of function and to guide rehabilitation milestones.

Neurological Conditions

In patients recovering from stroke, spinal cord injury, or demyelinating diseases such as multiple sclerosis. It can assist in goal setting for physical therapy and monitor motor recovery over time.

Chronic Musculoskeletal Disorders

Conditions such as osteoarthritis, chronic low back pain with radicular symptoms, tendinopathies, and plantar fasciitis often result in limitations in walking, climbing stairs, or standing for prolonged periods. LEFS serves as a reliable tracking tool to measure functional improvement or deterioration.

Sports-Related Injuries

In ligament injuries, overuse syndromes, or fractures, LEFS allows to assess for return to training or sport.
